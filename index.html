TWA Parts : Luxury vs Economy Division Case Study with Balanced Scorecards and Critical Evaluation

HBS Case 9-113-030 · Automotive Manufacturing
TWA Parts (TWAP)
A $6.6B Tier 1 auto parts manufacturer, subsidiary of a U.S. diversified manufacturing conglomerate. Facing industry downturn and near-insolvency of key customers (GM, Chrysler), CEO Ellen Bright closed the mid-priced and truck divisions to concentrate resources on two divisions: Luxury (serving European OEMs) and Economy (targeting Asia-based OEMs). Financial target: ROCE of 8% and positive cash flow by 2011, with a near-term 2009 goal of lifting ROCE from –15% to –7%.
Division Profiles
Luxury Division — Eckhard
Innovation & Technology for European OEMs

Target customers: European luxury OEMs (high-end car brands) who prioritise new product innovation, technical expertise, and premium design.

Core offerings: Electronics and interior components competing on technological superiority, product innovation, and design excellence.

Market positioning: Product innovator and technical leader. OEMs rate "new product innovation" and "product design" as their top two buying criteria.

Key challenges: High R&D and marketing spend; quality slippage emerging as a warning sign; only 10 of 20 targeted R&D partnerships secured.

Differentiation European luxury OEMs Quality risk
Economy Division — Kwon
Lowest Lifetime Cost for Asia-Based OEMs

Target customers: Asia-based economy OEMs targeting price-sensitive end consumers who rank "low cost" and "fuel economy" highest.

Core offerings: Electronics and interiors differentiated on high quality and lowest lifetime cost, not lowest initial price.

Market positioning: Quality-at-value leader. Competing against rivals who focus only on lowest upfront cost by offering superior durability and lower maintenance.

Key challenges: All financial targets missed in first 6 months; investing heavily in building operational capabilities before revenue benefits arrive.

Quality differentiation Asia growth markets Short-term losses

Q1 — Business Strategy of Each Division

Luxury Division (Eckhard) follows a differentiation through product innovation strategy. European luxury OEMs ( the division's primary customers ) rank "new product innovation" and "product design" as their top buying criteria (Exhibit 2). Eckhard's strategy directly reflects this: compete by being the technology leader, not the cheapest option. Revenue grows through innovation-led customer satisfaction, and the goal is to secure deep R&D partnerships that create switching costs and lock in long-term revenue. Cost management (raw materials reduction) is secondary, a means to improve margins, not the core competitive weapon.


Economy Division (Kwon) follows a quality-based cost leadership strategy, a deliberate contrast to competitors who focus on lowest initial purchase price. Kwon's insight is that Asia-based economy OEMs are ultimately hurt by poor-quality parts (warranty costs, field failures), so TWAP can win by offering the lowest total lifetime cost. This requires a long build-up: invest in TQM, lean manufacturing, JIT delivery, and supplier quality now so that the quality reputation and cost efficiency materialise over time. The strategy is fundamentally about building a durable competitive foundation, not maximising short-term margins.

Q2 — Comment on Strategy Maps & Scorecards — What Would You Change?

Luxury Division — What works: The financial perspective is well-constructed and logically connected to customer objectives. The innovation and customer satisfaction metrics are appropriate for a luxury OEM-facing division. Gross margin as a KPI makes sense given the premium positioning.


Luxury Division — What is weak: The strategy map is dangerously thin below the customer layer. With only one process objective (reduce raw materials cost) and one L&G objective (employee engagement), it fails to explain how innovation actually happens. For a division whose entire strategy rests on product leadership, there is no process-level objective for R&D cycle time, new product development, or quality control, yet defect rates are already slipping to 5 PPM against a 3 PPM target. The single L&G objective of "employee engagement" is generic and not causally linked to anything specific. It is not clear how higher engagement leads to more innovation.


What to change : Luxury: Add process objectives around new product development speed, R&D investment effectiveness, and quality management. Add L&G objectives for technical talent hiring and innovation skill development. The R&D partnership target of 20 customers was missed badly (only 10 achieved), this needed its own process initiative in the map, not just a customer-level metric.


Economy Division : What works: Kwon's strategy map is comprehensive and causally coherent. The L&G layer (TQM training, JIT expertise, IT alignment) directly enables the process improvements (defect elimination, supplier quality, demand forecasting), which in turn support the customer value proposition (lowest lifetime cost, quality reputation), which logically drives financial improvement. This is textbook balanced scorecard logic, well-executed.


Economy Division : What is weak: The scorecard is arguably too crowded. Seven process objectives and four L&G objectives make it difficult to prioritise. There is also no explicit linkage in the map showing how Asia market development (a geographic growth strategy) connects to the process and L&G investments, the map treats all markets as one. Additionally, the financial targets may have been set too ambitiously for a 6-month horizon given the scale of the transformation underway.


What to change : Economy: Consolidate process objectives into 4–5 highest-impact items. Add a customer objective specifically tied to Asia OEM acquisition, with a matching process objective around geographic expansion or localisation capabilities.

Q3 : Which Manager Prepared a Better Strategy Map & Scorecard?

Kwon (Economy Division) prepared a significantly better strategy map and balanced scorecard.


A good strategy map must tell a coherent story: here is what we need to learn → so we can improve how we operate → so we can serve customers better → so we generate financial results. Kwon's map does exactly this. Every layer connects to the next. The objectives are specific, the metrics are measurable, and the initiatives are funded and traceable.


Eckhard's map is structurally broken. A division competing on innovation and quality leadership has one process objective (cost reduction) and one L&G objective (engagement). There is no process-level plan for how innovation happens, no talent strategy beyond a vague engagement survey, and no linkage between L&G and the customer layer. When the defect rate slips and only half the R&D partnerships are secured, the scorecard gives no diagnostic insight into why — because the underlying drivers were never mapped.

Q4 — Which Division Manager Performed Better?

This is the tension at the heart of the case. The answer depends on the time horizon and what you believe performance really means.

Better short-term results
Eckhard — Luxury Division
ROCE: +4% vs –7% target (beat significantly). Cash flow: +$8M vs –$12M target. Gross margin: 15% vs 14% target. By pure financial metrics over 6 months, Eckhard clearly outperformed. However, quality slipped to 5 PPM (target: 3 PPM), only 10 of 20 R&D partnerships were secured, and employee engagement is critically low at 62%. These are leading indicators pointing to future trouble in an innovation-dependent business.
Better strategic execution
Kwon — Economy Division
Every financial target missed, ROCE at –12% vs –7%, cash flow –$14M vs –$12M. But nearly all process and L&G targets met or exceeded: defect rate at 4 PPM (beat 5 PPM target), suppliers 78% A-rated (beat 75%), EDI orders at 95% (beat 90%), preferred supplier relationships at 60 (beat target of 50), TQM and JIT certifications ahead of target. Kwon is building exactly the foundation the strategy requires.

Conclusion: On balance, Kwon performed better as a strategic manager, even though Eckhard produced better short-term financial results. The Economy Division's strategy explicitly requires upfront investment before financial payoff, the operational green lights indicate the strategy is on track. Eckhard's financial wins, by contrast, may have come partly at the expense of quality and talent investment, both of which are existential risks for a division that lives or dies by innovation. Ellen Bright should commend Eckhard on the financial results while issuing serious warnings on quality (5 PPM defects) and the R&D partnership shortfall. Kwon should be affirmed for the execution quality, but pressed on whether the financial lag is a timing issue or a deeper structural problem.
